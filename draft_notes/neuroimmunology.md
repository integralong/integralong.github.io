<!-- Related to the Week 2, DRP program, I wonder how these concepts can be applied to neuroimmunology.

**Constant vs Optimal therapy in neuroimmunology**
A. Constant therapy (traditional)
This is already exists in practice, and very similar to HIV therapy: effective but blunt.

B. Optimal therapy (control-based, adaptive)
This is where neuroimmunology is headed, not where it fully is yet.
"Treat neuroimmune activity as a controllable dynamic system rather than a static disease."

Key features:

* Time-dependent dosing

* State-feedback (biomarkers guide therapy)

* Objective functions (minimize inflammation + toxicity while maximizing neural preservation)

Mathematically, this is identical in spirit to optimal HIV control models.

Unique neuroimmunology complications.
This is where it differs from HIV:

A. The brain is not tolerant of error

Small overdosing → cognitive or psychiatric effects

Timing matters more than magnitude

B. Immune responses can be protective

Suppressing them continuously can block repair

Optimal control must allow inflammation at certain phases

C. Measurement is harder

Viral load ≈ clean scalar

Neuroimmune state ≈ noisy, multidimensional, delayed signals

So optimal therapy in neuroimmunology often needs:

surrogate biomarkers

probabilistic or robust control

delayed feedback models



Q. how ready these diseases are for optimal-control model?
Q. why constant therapy can worson long-term neuro outcomes?
Q. how many differ neuro and hiv? -->
